साङ्ख्य दर्शन — the philosophy of enumeration

The oldest of the six darshanas, attributed to Maharshi Kapila: a fearless counting of everything that exists, until the count ends and only the Witness remains.

What "Sankhya" means

The word saankhya comes from sankhyaa — number, enumeration, discriminative reckoning. Where other paths begin with worship or practice, Sankhya begins with an audit: what, exactly, exists? Its answer is famously precise — twenty-five tattvas, twenty-four of them belonging to nature, and one — the Purusha — standing apart as pure consciousness.

Sankhya is a dvaita of principle: two eternal, uncreated realities. Purusha — consciousness, the seer, plural (each jiva a distinct witness), never changing, never acting. Prakriti — one, unconscious, endlessly creative, the equilibrium of three gunas. The whole drama of existence, says Kapila, is what happens when these two come near each other.

दुःखत्रयाभिघाताज्जिज्ञासा तदभिघातके हेतौ ।
"From the torment of the three kinds of suffering arises the desire to know the means of ending them." — the very first line of the Sankhya Karika. Philosophy here is not curiosity; it is medicine.

The three gunas

Prakriti is woven of three strands — sattva (luminosity, clarity, lightness), rajas (movement, passion, restlessness) and tamas (inertia, darkness, heaviness). Nothing in nature — no mood, no object, no thought — is free of them; everything is a particular ratio of the three. Before creation they rest in perfect balance; creation is nothing but that balance disturbed.

Why it still matters

Sankhya's vocabulary quietly runs through all of Hindu thought. The Gita's second chapter is titled Sankhya Yoga. Ayurveda's account of body and mind, Yoga's psychology of chitta and its vrittis, the Bhagavata's cosmology — all stand on Kapila's enumeration. To study Sankhya is to be handed the grammar in which the rest of the shastras are written. That is exactly what made the 2006 satsang remarkable: it taught the grammar with the sweetness of a katha.
